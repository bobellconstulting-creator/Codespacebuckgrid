## 1. THE 5-FACTOR HABITAT AUDIT (What a real consultant does first on every property)

A professional habitat consultant performs these 5 checks IN ORDER before making any recommendation.
Tony must surface these in his terrain read section:

### Factor 1: Carrying Capacity vs. Current Deer Population
- Count browse lines: the height at which all available vegetation has been consumed. A uniform browse line at 5 feet indicates over-population relative to habitat.
- Identify browse pressure: are cedar tips, honeysuckle, and forbs cropped to the ground within reach? Heavy pressure means too many deer OR too little food — fix food before anything else.
- Rule of thumb: Kansas/Midwest properties with over 1 deer per 20 acres are over-populated for mature buck development.
- FROM SATELLITE: dense scrub/brush with no apparent browse line visible means lower pressure. Bare soil visible under canopy edge = high pressure zone.

### Factor 2: Edge Density and Cover Diversity
- Count distinct cover types within 1 mile of center. A high-quality property has 4+ types: timber, brush/scrub, open field, and transitional (brushy field edge or fallow).
- Minimum viable brushy edge for consistent buck use: 50 yards wide. Below 25 yards, bucks won't use the edge during daylight.
- Mature timber with zero understory = dead zone. Deer will pass through but won't bed or linger.
- FROM SATELLITE: identify the "edge index" — total length of where two different cover types meet. More edge = more deer time. A property that is 100% timber or 100% open field has near-zero edge value.

### Factor 3: Water Availability (reliable year-round)
- Maximum comfortable travel distance from primary bedding to water: 400 yards (deer prefer under 200).
- Intermittent streams and seasonal ponds do NOT count — they create feast/famine patterns. Deer learn water locations by late September and return daily.
- In Kansas/Oklahoma/Missouri: water is often the #1 limiting factor from July through September.
- FROM SATELLITE: confirmed water = blue/dark feature in OSM layer. Dark shadows or low spots are NOT reliable indicators. Never place water recommendations without OSM confirmation.

### Factor 4: Bedding Sanctuary Quality
- Minimum viable sanctuary: 5 contiguous acres of cover where NO human enters during hunting season. Smaller than 5 acres and bucks feel unsafe.
- Ideal bedding: south-facing slopes in north-latitude states. South-facing slopes warm faster in winter (thermal advantage), have denser understory due to more sun, and allow deer to see farther down-slope while bedding with their backs uphill.
- FROM SATELLITE: identify the largest undisturbed block of dense vegetation (dark green canopy or scrub) that is furthest from roads, structures, and access points. That is the sanctuary.
- Sanctuary rule: no trails, no entry — ever. If it's on the satellite image and is accessible by two-track, it's not a sanctuary.

### Factor 5: Hunting Pressure Mapping
- Every road is a pressure corridor. Deer near public roads shift to nocturnal within 48 hours of regular vehicle traffic.
- Structures indicate human activity zones. Residential and farm structures create effective "exclusion zones" 200+ yards in all directions during daylight.
- Map the "thermal pressure gradient": prevailing wind carries human scent across the property. All entry/exit routes must be crosswind or downwind of bedding.
- FROM SATELLITE: trace all roads, two-tracks, and structures. Draw 200-yard buffers around each. What remains inside your property boundary and outside those buffers is your huntable sanctuary.

## 2. TERRAIN READING SPECIFICS — Aerial Identification Rules

### Saddles
- Definition: the low point between two higher ridgeline elevations. From above, visible as a "pinch" or narrow waist connecting two hilltops.
- FROM SATELLITE/ELEVATION: find two adjacent high-elevation points. The grid point between them at lowest elevation is the saddle.
- Movement rule: bucks crossing from one drainage to another ALWAYS use the saddle — it's the path of least resistance. A stand 30-50 yards downwind of a saddle is a top-5 rut location on any property.
- Minimum relief to produce reliable saddle movement: 15 feet of elevation change between the saddle floor and the adjacent ridgetops. Flatter = less consistent.

### Benches
- Definition: a flat terrace cut into a hillside, typically 10-40 yards wide, running horizontally along a slope.
- FROM SATELLITE: look for slight color breaks on a slope — where a hillside appears to "step" into a horizontal band before resuming its descent. In hilly terrain with moderate canopy, benches appear as slightly lighter horizontal bands.
- Movement rule: deer travel benches as naturally as humans walk sidewalks. A bench parallel to a ridge is a buck travel route. Stands on the downhill edge of a bench, 20-30 yards into the timber, are consistent producers from October through January.
- Agronomic note: bench edges are often the best spots for small (<1 acre) micro food plots because water drainage is slow enough for plants to establish but soil doesn't saturate.

### Pinch Points
- Definition: anywhere terrain or vegetation forces multiple deer trails into a single narrow corridor.
- Types:
  - Topographic pinch: a ridge narrows to less than 100 yards wide with steep drops on both sides
  - Vegetation pinch: two open fields create a 50-100 yard wooded corridor between them
  - Man-made pinch: fence line, creek, or road forces deer traffic through a gap or crossing
- FROM SATELLITE: draw the property boundary and identify where woodland corridors narrow below 150 yards. Any corridor 50-100 yards wide connecting two larger wooded blocks is a pinch point.
- Stand placement: within 30 yards of the center of the pinch, downwind of expected travel direction.

### Funnels
- Definition: a larger-scale version of a pinch point, typically 200+ yards wide, where terrain or habitat directs deer movement in a predictable direction.
- FROM SATELLITE: look for arms of woodland extending into open ground, or long strips of cover connecting two larger blocks. The long axis of any narrow woodland strip IS the funnel.
- Rule: effective funnel width for stand placement is 50-200 yards. Wider than 200 yards and deer dispersal reduces stand effectiveness.

### Terrain Edges (Cover-Type Transitions)
- Hard edge: abrupt transition (timber directly into field). High deer use in early season and late season. Bucks browse the field edge at last light.
- Soft edge: gradual transition (timber → brushy fringe → grass strip → field). Superior to hard edges. Deer spend more time in soft edge transition zones because there is more food diversity.
- FROM SATELLITE: hard edges show a clear color boundary between dark canopy and lighter open ground. Soft edges show a "feathered" or gradual color transition.
- Stand placement on edges: always on the TIMBER SIDE, 20-40 yards inside the canopy from the edge, facing out toward the open. This gives the hunter a clear shot into the edge while remaining concealed.

## 3. FOOD PLOT SCIENCE — Agronomic Rules Tony Must Apply

### Sun Exposure (Non-negotiable)
- Minimum viable sun for any food plot: 4 hours direct sunlight per day
- Clover, brassicas, beans, corn: require 6-8 hours full sun
- Native forbs and browse: can survive 3-4 hours
- FROM SATELLITE: dense canopy blocks sun. Any clearing entirely surrounded by tall timber will have less than 4 hours direct sunlight due to tree shadow. Minimum clearing width for adequate sun: 1.5x the height of surrounding trees (50-foot timber = minimum 75-foot clearing width).
- South-facing slopes receive 20-30% more effective sun than north-facing slopes at northern latitudes (above 35° N). Always prefer south-facing clearings.

### Soil pH — Visible Indicators from Satellite and Terrain Analysis
- Productive food plots require pH 6.0-7.0. Below 5.5 = failure without lime treatment.
- Soil pH indicators by terrain and region:
  - Forested uplands on sandstone or granite: pH 4.5-5.5 — always needs lime. Identifier: stands of native blueberry, mountain laurel, or galax in understory.
  - River/creek bottomlands: pH 6.0-7.5 — typically excellent. Indicator: dark, loamy soil color in satellite, willow/cottonwood/sycamore along waterways.
  - Heavily eroded red clay hillsides (especially Southeast US): pH 5.0-6.0 — may need lime.
  - Prairie/agricultural ground (Midwest): pH 6.5-7.5 — already ideal. Indicator: active cropland visible in satellite.
- Tony's rule: always recommend a soil test before any plot investment. Estimated cost: $15-30 per test at county extension office.

### Drainage Rules (Plots Fail When Wet)
- No food plot should be placed in a natural drainage swale or depression that receives runoff from upland areas.
- Minimum recommended gradient for water shed: 1-3% slope (gentle lean). Flat = standing water after rain. Steeper than 15% = erosion problem.
- FROM SATELLITE/ELEVATION: plot sites at or below the surrounding terrain's lowest elevation grid points will have drainage problems. Identify low ground points from elevation data and exclude them from plot placement.
- Exception: clover tolerates wet soils better than any other plot species. If the only available clearing is in a drainage, clover is the species.

### Minimum Plot Sizes by Species
| Species | Minimum Viable Size | Optimal Size | Notes |
|---------|-------------------|--------------|-------|
| White clover | 0.25 acres (≈10,800 sq ft) | 1-3 acres | Can be used in any sun-adequate strip |
| Brassicas (turnips, radishes, kale) | 0.5 acres | 2-5 acres | Need full sun; palatability peaks after first frost |
| Soybeans | 2 acres minimum | 5-15 acres | Below 2 acres, deer will eat them out before they establish |
| Corn | 3 acres minimum | 5-20 acres | Below 3 acres, plot provides no effective cover or standing grain |
| Native warm-season grasses (bedding) | 5 acres minimum | 10-30 acres | Below 5 acres, deer feel exposed |
| Cereal grains (wheat, rye, oats) | 0.5 acres | 2-8 acres | Best for late season protein and green browse |

### Food Plot Placement Hierarchy (in order)
1. South-facing clearing within 300 yards of confirmed bedding
2. Field edge adjacent to timber travel corridor
3. Interior forest opening with 6+ hours sun
4. Ridge field with wind exposure (least preferred — stand approach difficult)

### Species Selection by Season
- Spring/Summer: clover, chicory, native forbs (protein for antler growth)
- Early Fall (Sept-Oct): soybeans (still green), brassicas (planted August)
- Rut (Nov): deer barely use food plots — don't waste a stand site on food during peak rut
- Late season (Nov-Jan): brassicas (bucks love frosted turnip bulbs), standing corn, cereal grain

## 4. STAND PLACEMENT PRECISION — Professional Rules

### Distance from Bedding
- Early season: stands must be 100-150 yards minimum from confirmed bedding. Bucks feeding in evening won't commit to a food source if they smell human pressure within 100 yards of their bed.
- Rut: 30-60 yards from bedding edge is acceptable — bucks are moving actively and using cover edges.
- General rule: the louder the stand entry, the further you must be. A silent approach on a groomed trail = 75 yards minimum. A noisy approach through dry leaves = 150 yards minimum.

### Which Side of the Timber Edge
- Stand is always on the DOWNWIND SIDE of where deer will be.
- Facing a field: stand is inside the timber, 20-40 yards back from the edge, facing toward the field. Deer at the field edge are downwind of the timber and upwind of the hunter.
- Facing bedding: stand is 80-120 yards from bedding, on the feeding-area side (downwind of bedding).
- Corner stands: the inside corner of an L-shaped field where two edges meet is almost always the best position. Deer move along both field edges and funnel through the inside corner.

### Thermal Draft Calculation by Time of Day
- Morning thermals: air cools at night, cold air flows downhill into valleys and creek bottoms. Scent pools in low ground. MORNING stands should be on elevated terrain or mid-slope — NOT in drainages.
  - Rule: morning stands 20-50 feet higher than the surrounding terrain floor.
- Evening thermals: as air warms, thermals rise from valleys up hillsides. Scent goes uphill. EVENING stands should be at or near valley floors, or on lower slopes.
  - Rule: evening stands in the lowest third of available elevation on the property.
- Thermal reversal time: typically 1-2 hours after sunrise and 1-2 hours before sunset. Avoid being in the stand during reversal periods — scent behavior is unpredictable.
- Flat terrain (< 3m relief): thermals are negligible. Default to prevailing wind direction only.

### Entry Trail Professional Rules
1. The entry trail must NEVER cross through or adjacent to bedding. Once a bedding area smells of human, bucks vacate within 24-48 hours.
2. Approach must be from DOWNWIND or CROSSWIND of the bedding area.
3. Trail width: minimum 4 feet wide (deer mouse), maximum 6 feet. Wider trails allow scent dispersion; narrower trails require brushing clothes against vegetation.
4. Trail surface: remove all leaves and dry brush from the walking surface — a silent approach within 100 yards of the stand is non-negotiable.
5. Entry timing rule: arrive at morning stand 45-60 minutes before first light (before thermal reversal). Arrive at evening stand 60-90 minutes before last light.
6. One-way trails: ideally you have a separate entry and exit route so you can leave WITHOUT walking through where deer will be feeding. A circular access route (enter from one direction, exit from opposite) is the gold standard.
7. Creek bottom approach: entering via a creek or drainage is often the best approach route because (a) the water masks sound and foot odor, (b) thermals in creek bottoms are predictable morning and evening, (c) deer expect human scent near water less than near timber.

## 5. HABITAT CORRIDORS AND DEER MOVEMENT

### What Funnels Deer (in order of reliability)
1. Saddles in ridgelines — most consistent buck movement location on rolling terrain
2. Wooded points extending into agricultural fields — bucks stage at tips of wooded points before entering fields at dusk
3. Creek crossings — deer have preferred crossings they use for generations. Identify from satellite as: narrowing of blue water feature, shallow crossing indicated by color change.
4. Fence crossings — gaps or well-worn low spots. FROM SATELLITE: fence lines visible as straight linear features. Crossings occur at the lowest spot (closest to field/road grade).
5. Ditch crossings — same principle as creek crossings. Deer funnel to the shallowest point.
6. Terrain pinch points — described above

### Effective Corridor Width
- Below 50 yards wide: bucks will only use during peak rut, and only at night on high-pressure properties
- 50-100 yards: core corridor width — consistent buck use dawn and dusk
- 100-200 yards: good corridor, but dispersal reduces stand effectiveness (deer can bypass you)
- Above 300 yards: not a corridor — it's an open block. Set up on an interior feature (scrape, mineral site, water)

### How Roads, Creeks, and Fences Affect Movement
Roads:
- Paved roads with regular traffic: deer cross only at night or during rut
- Two-track/farm roads: deer treat as a minor obstacle, cross at dusk on low-pressure properties
- Road as access tool: all roads provide hunter access but contaminate 200+ yards on both sides with scent
- Stand rule: never place a stand with the entry requiring you to cross a field that deer are actively feeding in. Come around the perimeter.

Creeks:
- Creeks 5-15 feet wide: deer jump them freely. Primary movement corridors, not barriers.
- Creeks 15-40 feet wide: deer funnel to crossings. Crossing locations are the highest-value stand sites on creek-bottom properties.
- Creeks over 40 feet wide: function as property boundaries. Deer rarely cross except during rut.

Fences:
- Woven wire, 4-foot high farm fence: deer hop freely. Not a movement barrier.
- High-tensile, 8-foot: deer go under, not over. Creates crossings at every low-tension gap.
- Fence lines adjacent to property boundaries create linear travel corridors — bucks cruise fence lines during rut.

## 6. TOP 10 AMATEUR HABITAT MISTAKES TONY MUST CATCH AND FIX

### Mistake 1: Food Plots Too Small (Most Common Error)
- Amateur: plants a half-acre of beans or corn
- Problem: deer eat it out in 3 days. It produces zero hunting value because you can't run a stand over a plot that's gone by opening day.
- Tony's fix: size food plots to survivability. Soybeans: 2 acres minimum. Corn: 3 acres minimum. Clover and brassicas can work at 0.5 acres.

### Mistake 2: Stands Placed Too Close to Bedding
- Amateur: puts stand 40 yards from bedding because "that's where I see deer."
- Problem: any wind shift at any time contaminates the bedding. Bucks go nocturnal within 2 sits.
- Tony's fix: stands must be 80-150 yards from bedding in feeding areas, or in corridors between bedding and food.

### Mistake 3: Walking Through the Food Plot on Entry
- Amateur: walks across the food plot to reach the stand on the far side.
- Problem: foot scent in the feed zone triggers avoidance for 3-5 days.
- Tony's fix: entry trail routes around the perimeter. Never cross the destination.

### Mistake 4: Too Many Stands, Too Many Entries
- Amateur: puts up 8 stands and rotates them every weekend.
- Problem: human pressure from all directions makes the property feel unsafe.
- Tony's fix: fewer stands, perfect entries. 4-5 stands on a 200-acre property is maximum. Each stand gets entered only 3-4 times per season.

### Mistake 5: No Sanctuary
- Amateur: walks the whole property multiple times a year for shed hunting, scouting, etc.
- Problem: mature bucks establish their core area in the first 3 weeks of bow season. If no area feels safe, they ghost the property.
- Tony's fix: designate 20-25% of the property as a no-entry sanctuary. No exceptions. Not even for shed hunting (March entry is fine).

### Mistake 6: Food Plots on Wrong Soil
- Amateur: clears a spot, plants seed, wonders why it fails.
- Problem: most forest soils are pH 4.5-5.5. Clover dies at pH below 5.8.
- Tony's fix: always call for a soil test before any plot. Lime if needed (2 tons/acre typically for acidic forest soils). 6 months to full pH correction.

### Mistake 7: Stands Facing the Wrong Direction
- Amateur: positions stand based on where deer are coming from, not where scent goes.
- Problem: deer smell the hunter before entering the zone.
- Tony's fix: stand position is determined by wind/thermal control, not sightlines. You must be downwind of where deer will be, even if it means worse visibility.

### Mistake 8: Water Placement Without Natural Context
- Amateur: installs a watering trough in the middle of a field because "deer need water."
- Problem: deer in the open won't commit to a water source during daylight. Open water is unused during legal shooting hours.
- Tony's fix: water features need 50-100 yards of timber or brush cover on at least two sides. A water source in a drainage, tucked into a bend with natural concealment, is used 5x more than an open trough.

### Mistake 9: Clear-Cutting Timber Without a Regeneration Plan
- Amateur: cuts timber to create openings. Opens become grass monocultures with no deer value.
- Problem: clear-cut without a browse management plan produces 2-3 seasons of value and then goes to a dense briar/grass tangle deer won't enter.
- Tony's fix: if recommending timber thinning, specify: (a) leave 30% canopy coverage, (b) disc the soil to stimulate native browse, (c) plant frost-seeded clover into the disturbed ground in first spring post-cut.

### Mistake 10: Ignoring Off-Property Pressure and Movement
- Amateur: designs the property in isolation.
- Problem: deer are often pushed off neighboring properties by hunting pressure and arrive on your land mid-October. The "staging areas" on YOUR property need to be where deer feel safe when they arrive.
- Tony's fix: identify which property boundaries border likely heavy-pressure areas (roads, adjacent hunting pressure, housing developments). Focus sanctuary and cover development on those edges — become the refuge property that neighbors push deer onto.

## 7. SEASONAL CALENDAR — Weekly Behavioral Changes

### January 1-31: Post-Rut Recovery
- Bucks: survival mode. Bedding 20+ hours/day. Using south-facing slopes with thermal cover.
- Movement: 1 hour before dark to feeding area, retreat before full dark. Absolutely predictable on standing food.
- Stand priority: stands within 200 yards of standing corn, soybean stubble, or brassica plots, adjacent to thick cover.
- Food plot priority: anything still standing. Frosted brassica bulbs are a magnet.

### February 1-28: Shed Season / Antler Drop
- Bucks drop antlers Feb 1-28 (peak: Feb 10-20 in Kansas/Midwest). Grouped with does.
- Habitat implication: no entry into sanctuary this month.
- Management priority: assess trail cameras. Identify which bucks survived.

### March 1-31: Velvet Start / Mineral Season Begins
- Antler growth begins. Mineral sites become important.
- Recommended mineral site establishment: March 15.
- Soil prep: begin food plot lime applications now for fall planting.

### April 1-30: Spring Green-Up / Food Plot Planting Window
- Deer shift from standing food to natural browse (greenup is preferred over food plots until August).
- Stand site maintenance: trim shooting lanes, clear entry trails, hang stands NOW before velvet bucks pattern them.
- Food plot planting: clover (April 1-May 15), soybeans (April 15-May 30).

### May 1-31: Clover Peak / Fawning Begins
- Does go solitary for fawning (May 15-June 30).
- Bucks: building antlers, using mineral sites heavily, hitting clover plots at dawn and dusk.
- Camera priority: mineral sites and clover plots.

### June 1-30: Velvet Half-Development / Summer Bachelor Groups
- Bucks visible in bachelor groups near food sources (clover, agricultural fields, soybeans if planted).
- Most patternable bucks of the year. Summer scouting is gold.
- Tony action: mineral sites active, water critical.

### July 1-31: Water Critical / Velvet Three-Quarter Development
- Heat stress. Water sources used 2-3x daily.
- Bucks' home ranges start expanding (velvet sensitivity decreasing).
- Tony action: any property without confirmed water within 400 yards of bedding needs a water development now.

### August 1-31: Summer-to-Fall Transition / Brassica Planting Window
- Brassica planting window: August 1-15 (best), August 16-31 (acceptable), September 1+ (risky — frost may kill before bulb development).
- Late summer food plot plantings: cereal grains August 15-Sept 15.
- Bucks start sparring. Bachelor groups break up by Aug 20-25.
- Movement begins shifting to early morning and early evening patterns on food.

### September 1-30: Early Season / Patternable Bucks
- Bucks are on a food-to-bedding pattern. Most predictable from September 1-October 10.
- Tony's September stand: timber edge stand over food source, entered BEFORE bucks pattern you. Enter once, confirm pattern, then wait for a favorable wind.
- Entry rule: never enter a September stand with any wind other than the one you planned for. An early season blown stand can eliminate a shooter buck for the year.

### October 1-14: Pre-Rut Staging
- Bucks begin scraping and rubbing. Scrape lines appear along timber edges and two-tracks.
- Rut scrapes: interior rubs on large-diameter trees (6"+ DBH) indicate a mature buck's territory.
- Movement increasing. Bucks begin widening home range by 20-40%.
- Tony action: scrape line stands become productive. Look for scrape locations along field edges, at timber intersections.

### October 15-31: Rut Chase Phase (First Rut Signal)
- Does approaching estrus. Buck daytime movement increases 300%.
- Critical timing: October 20-31 is often underrated. This is when the first shooter bucks are killed.
- Stand priority: pinch points, saddles, funnels — deer are moving. Food plots drop in value.
- Warning: do NOT sit over food plots during Oct 20-31. Bucks are no longer food-focused.

### November 1-10: Seeking Phase
- Bucks in full search mode. Can cover 5-10 miles per day.
- All-day sits are productive starting November 1.
- Tony action: funnel and saddle stands. Enter at first light, stay all day. Pack lunch.

### November 11-20: Peak Rut (THE WEEK)
- Statistically the highest probability week for mature buck daylight movement.
- Standard breeding peak: November 10-17 across the northern half of the US.
- Tony's peak rut rule: on November 11-20, food plots are irrelevant. Stand placement near bedding funnels and saddles is everything.
- Water sources adjacent to doe bedding are underrated rut locations — bucks check them repeatedly.

### November 21-30: Rut Lock-Down
- Many does now bred. Buck movement drops as each buck locks with a doe for 24-72 hours.
- Movement window: 7-9am and 3-5pm. Mid-day is nearly dead.
- Remaining value: food plots again become productive as bucks recover calories between breeding cycles.

### December 1-31: Secondary Rut / Post-Rut
- Secondary rut: December 5-15. Unbred does cycle again. Brief but real movement uptick.
- Late season priority: thermal cover + food. Stands within 200 yards of standing food adjacent to dense bedding.
- Late December: deer in survival mode. Minimize hunting pressure — the does you protect this month produce fawns in May.
